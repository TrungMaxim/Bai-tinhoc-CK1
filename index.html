WESTERN CLASSICAL MUSIC IN LATE-ROMANTICISM & THE EARLY TWENTIETH CENTURY

A brief history

The late 19th and early 20th centuries were a tumultuous and transformative period for Western classical music, marking a decisive move away from the established traditions of the Romantic era toward the radical experiments of Modernism. This period was characterized by a profound shift in musical ideology, where composers questioned the foundational principles of composition that had governed Western music for centuries, especially the system of tonality. Driven by a desire for fresh expression, a rejection of the emotional saturation of Late Romanticism, and influenced by parallel movements in art and literature, musicians sought new sonic languages. This was not a unified march into one new style, but rather a vibrant splintering into multiple, often contradictory, directions—Impressionism, Expressionism, and various national schools—all vying to define the sound of the new century. The quest for originality and a complete break from the past often led composers to explore non-Western scales, revive medieval modes, and, most controversially, abandon the tonic-dominant hierarchy altogether.

One of the first significant departures from German-centric Romanticism was the rise of Impressionism in France, spearheaded by Claude Debussy and followed by Maurice Ravel. Debussy, in particular, sought to evoke moods, atmospheres, and sensations rather than express overt emotion or adhere to strict narrative programs. This approach drew inspiration from Impressionist painters like Monet, valuing the fleeting "impression" over detailed representation. To achieve this, Impressionist composers deliberately strayed from traditional tonality. Debussy utilized non-functional harmony, where chords were often used for their timbre (tone color) and not to drive harmonic progression towards a resolution. He frequently employed exotic scales like the whole-tone scale (which lacks the half-steps necessary for a strong sense of tonic) and the pentatonic scale, creating a sense of harmonic ambiguity and fluidity. This blurring of the tonal center and emphasis on orchestral color—using instruments like the harp, muted brass, and divided strings in novel ways—created a sound world described as misty, dreamy, and evocative, as heard in seminal works like Debussy's Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune and Ravel's Jeux d'eau. . Across Europe, Russian composer Alexander Scriabin (1872–1915) followed a highly individual and mystical path that also undermined traditional tonality, though independently of the French Impressionists. Scriabin's journey from a Chopin-influenced Late Romantic style led to a unique, increasingly dissonant language governed by his own metaphysical and synesthetic beliefs. His later works, like Prometheus: The Poem of Fire, utilized a complex, self-derived sonority known as the "Mystic Chord" (a six-note chord built on fourths), creating a highly chromatically dense and ecstatic sound that was not entirely atonal but definitely transcended the common practice period's definition of key, pushing harmonic boundaries to their breaking point.

Simultaneously, a more radical break was formalized in Vienna by Arnold Schoenberg and his students, Alban Berg and Anton Webern, collectively known as the Second Viennese School. Driven by the artistic ideology of Expressionism, which sought to express intense, subjective, and often turbulent inner psychological states, Schoenberg felt that the late Romantic trend of perpetual chromaticism had fully exhausted the possibilities of the traditional tonal system. He declared the "Emancipation of the Dissonance," asserting that dissonant chords no longer needed to resolve to consonant ones, allowing them to function as independent, expressive entities. This led to a period of atonality (or 'pantonal' music), characterized by a lack of a central key, as seen in works like Schoenberg's Pierrot Lunaire. Realizing that total freedom often led to structural chaos, Schoenberg later systematized his atonal language by developing the Twelve-Tone Technique (or Dodecaphony) around 1923. This method uses a specific, ordered sequence of all twelve notes of the chromatic scale (the tone row) as the basis for a composition, ensuring that no single pitch is emphasized enough to establish a tonal center, thus maintaining an egalitarian and atonal relationship between all notes.

Yet, amidst these revolutionary movements, the grand, emotionally expansive style of Late Romanticism persisted, championed by composers who believed in the continued expressive power of tonality. Figures like Sergei Rachmaninoff, Nikolai Medtner, Sergei Bortkiewicz, and later, Erich Wolfgang Korngold (particularly in his film scores), upheld the ideals of Tchaikovsky and Mahler. They continued to write in a lush, harmonically rich, and lyrical style, demonstrating that the appeal of a clear emotional narrative, soaring melody, and functional harmony was far from dead. Rachmaninoff, often described as the "last great Romantic," composed powerful works like his Second Piano Concerto and Symphonic Dances well into the 20th century, cementing the co-existence of radically new and staunchly traditional compositional approaches in the modernist era.

Samuil Feinberg

May 26, 1890 - Oct 22, 1962

Russian and Soviet composer and pianist. The first pianist to perform the complete The Well-Tempered Clavier by Bach in concert in the USSR.

Nikolai Roslavets

Jan 4, 1881 - Aug 23, 1944

Modernist composer active in the Russian Empire and later the Soviet Union. He was a convinced modernist and cosmopolitan thinker; his music was officially suppressed from 1930 onwards.

Nikolai Obukhov

Apr 22, 1892 - Jun 13, 1954

Modernist and mystic Russian composer, active mainly in France. An avant-garde figure who took as his point of departure the late music of Scriabin. Inventor of the instrument named “Croix Sonore”.
